What is the growth mindset?

In 2006 American psychologist Carol Dweck published Mindset: The New Psychology of Success which discussed learning how to fulfill potential. In it, she discusses her coined term - the 'growth mindset'. Growth mindset describes a way of thinking about challenges and obstacles. They view them as opportunities, embrace constructive feedback, and know that their skills can always be improved.

The opposite is described as a fixed mindset; someone who avoids failure and believes their skills are static.

I was surprised by the pushback on Dweck's observation, and continued studies to support her theory. Overall, I think it's a positive outlook on learning that can positively influence people who may struggle with negative thinking.

This blog post had some good bullet points that helped solidify the difference in a fixed vs growth mindset.

What is Neuroplasticity?

Neuroplasticity the proof that we can change or improve our knowledge to enhance our skills.

The word itself describes the brain's ability to form and rewire neural connections in response to learning and experience. The more we practice and read about a specific topic, the stronger our brain's connection to that ability becomes. The pathway gets stronger and it becomes easier to recall. Even if it gets harder as we age, it still remains malleable.

Understanding this concept lets us know that growth mindset is real - that you aren't stuck 'being an idiot' or 'don't know enough'. That we can change our skills, and we can get better.

Repitition, reading more books, studies, articles - these all help with neuroplasticity. But keeping your brain happy and healthy also helps. Things like mindfulness, proper sleep and nutrition, and exercise can all help with your brain's neuroplasticity.

During my own learning, I continuously want to go back to the CSS on my website and see what other properties I could use. When I need a break from my current learning, I go back and take a look at the box model again, or the different ways people use flexbox. I reuse the terminal frequently to help memorise common CLI commands, fix bugs in my HTML and Google 'why do I even need this' to remind myself why I put certain tags in.

I also make sure to eat and break, lay down, and exercise every other day to fuel my brain and body.

All of these habits help grow the neuroplasticity in my brain, and helps with my memory recall. If you want to read more about neuroplasticity, I found a simple explanation here which describes how you can explain and apply it with kids while they're learning.

Incorporating with my learning plan

As I continue to learn, as I struggle, it's important to remember to be kind and open-minded towards learning, and to make sure I follow the self-care set in my learning plan.
