How I problem solve

During sprint 4 of my course we learnt a lot about different problem-solving methods; pseuodo-coding, rubber ducky method, and throwing stuff at the wall until it sticks (or using console.log).

My favourite has always been roping in unsuspecting victims to be my soundboard. Back when I was working with SharePoint Lists, I often used Microsoft's Power Apps to help automate the database. When I got stuck, I asked my coworker if she could help me when she had 5 minutes to spare.

I explained it best I could with an analogy. I told her there was essentially a cupboard, but when I went to open it, the door didn't open. I then asked what she would do first.

Whenever she answered, I took the response and tried to think about how that would work in code. If I was stuck in even beginning to figure out the pseudo code this really helped me find a starting point. Other times she just solved my issues. No matter how basic or simple something seemed to her, the overwhelm I felt seemed to block me from the answer. I always felt grateful when she made time to help me.

Elegant problem solving

Pseudo coding is my next favourite thing. However I mostly keep it mental or on a scrap of paper; sometimes it helps me better.

During my JavaScript Kata I found myself a little stuck on the best way to reverse a string. Basically taking a word and spelling it backwards.

In my head I went through the steps. Step 1, I would write a line of code. Step 2, I would add to the line of code, and so on. While I did solve the problem, the result was one line of pretty ugly code. So I went back and refactored it.

With the problem-solving out of the way, I just had to make it more readable for the next person who might come along and read my code. It's pretty satisfying to see your code looking neat and easy to read.

Reflecting on the different techniques

Pseudocode I do this mentally with confidence. I feel less confident implementing this in writing.
Trying something I love this one - paired with console.log it's super fun. I am confident with this.
Rubber ducky method I occasionally use this, less so for coding. I'm confident I can use this one though.
Reading error messages Confident with this, less so in the testing area since the formatting was new to me.
Google Search Engines, MDN, and StackOverflow have always been my best friends.
Asking peers/coaches for help If I am close with them it's fine to make a mistake. Getting over the embarrassment takes work.
Reflection Not too confident with this method. I do like using it to look further into other methods & properties though.
